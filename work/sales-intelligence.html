The commercial imperative

Your best accounts are the ones reps have no time to chase

Reps spend only 35% of their time actually selling. The rest goes to manual research, prospecting and CRM admin. In enterprise ABM that hurts most, because the accounts worth winning are the ones that need the most research: a Tier 1 enterprise deal averages $1.49M, 21 times a Tier 4 account, but takes a 126-day cycle, 3.4 times longer, across many senior stakeholders.

The gap surfaced during Indeed Flex's first company-wide ABM programme. Marketing served thought-leadership to target accounts and told sales "Company X is engaging". Sales could not act on it. The insight was missing the one thing reps needed most: who to call.

What I built

An AI teammate that does the research, so reps do the selling

I built a multi-agent system the sales team named "The Dude". It handles the three jobs reps dislike and AI is genuinely good at: finding the real decision-makers, enriching them with verified contact detail and live company signals, and scoring every account against Indeed Flex's ICP. Reps trigger it from Slack; it writes straight into the CRM.

It stops where judgment starts. The system surfaces intelligence; reps own the outreach and the relationship. That was a deliberate design choice, not a limitation. Augmentation, not automation. Framing it that way is what won executive sponsorship and, more importantly, buy-in from reps who could have seen it as a threat.

Capability 01

Precision targeting

Surfaces the decision-makers most likely to convert against HR, Ops and Procurement ICP criteria, and filters the rest out before a rep sees them.

Capability 02

Live buying signals

Recent news, funding, and hiring activity from Perplexity, LinkedIn and Indeed job posts, turned into talking points a rep can open with.

Capability 03

Explainable ICP scoring

Every contact gets a 1–5 score with a written reason, so reps can trust it and RevOps can tune it.

Capability 04

Native to the workflow

Runs in Slack and writes to HubSpot and Salesforce. No new app, no login, no context switch.

Design decision · Decentralised agents

The CRM is the shared memory, not a single orchestrator

Each agent runs independently and reads and writes to the CRM as persistent state. That avoids the context-window degradation that breaks centralised systems under high volume, lets me update one agent without touching the rest, and leaves a clean audit trail for every enriched record.

The engine

Score first, pay later: 7x cheaper enrichment

Traditional BI tools charge per seat or per bulk download, which rewards pulling huge untargeted lists. I inverted the model. Free biographical data feeds an LLM that scores each contact 1–5 against the ICP; only contacts scoring 4 or above trigger paid enrichment. That one design choice cut cost per enriched contact by 7x while cutting the noise reps had to wade through.

Adoption

The 20x unlock was UX, not the model

The first version lived on a separate web page with an open chat box. Despite training sessions and demos, it stalled at about 20 requests a week. Reps had to leave their workflow, remember a URL, and guess what to ask.

So I moved the trigger into Slack, the tool reps already use all day. Requests climbed from 20 to over 400 a week, a 20x jump, with no change to the AI underneath. For a sales org, that is the whole lesson: a technically superior system nobody opens is worth nothing.

Making it stick

Four trade-offs that decided adoption

Getting a sales org to adopt AI is a change-management problem before it is a technical one. Four calls shaped the result.

Universal
01 · Data access

Per-seat licences → one AI interface

Fewer than 30% of BI seats were ever used. Agents call the APIs for everyone, so every rep gets Apollo and Cognism through Slack, paying per use, not per seat.

7× lower
02 · Enrichment cost

Enrich everything → score first

Free data scores fit; only 4/5+ contacts get paid enrichment. Coverage held while cost per contact dropped 7x.

Trust
03 · Control

Full automation → human in the loop

Every request and CRM upload is rep-triggered and approved. That protected lead attribution, GDPR compliance and, above all, trust.

20×
04 · Adoption

Standalone dashboard → Slack

Moving the trigger into a tool reps already use drove a 20x jump in weekly use, with no change to the model.
