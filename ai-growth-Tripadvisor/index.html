Which numbers I'd stand on, and which I wouldn't

Trust the causal metric. Question the rest.

Metrics I trust most
Incrementality (clean markets)

It comes from a holdout, so it measures cause, not correlation. In the UK and Germany it is the one number that separates real growth from subsidised habit.

The direction of net revenue

Positive versus negative tracks incrementality closely, which is a good internal consistency check. I trust the ranking even before I trust the exact figure.

CVR lift as a supporting signal

Useful alongside incrementality, and it agrees with the story: lift is highest on first purchase (+22% UK) and lowest on repeat (+7% to +8%).

Metrics I'd question
France and Italy incrementality

Flagged for targeting issues in the holdout. I would not rank, cut or roll anything out on these until the test is rerun. Directional means directional.

Redemption rate on its own

UK repeat has the highest redemption (21%) and nearly the lowest incrementality. High redemption with low incrementality is the definition of wasted spend, not success.

"Net revenue" as a label

Positive / Marginal / Negative is a bucket, not a number. I want the actual contribution margin and the assumptions behind it before I bank a saving.

Everything, without a sample size

No n and no confidence intervals are shown. A "Positive" on a small cell can be noise. I'd attach significance before treating any single row as settled.

1 Part One · Before I recommend a Q3 strategy

Six things I'd ask for, and how each one changes the call.

I would not finalise a plan on this table alone. Each of these would move a specific decision, not just add detail. If a data point cannot change what I do, I don't wait for it.

💰

Customer lifetime value

First booking value is a snapshot. A discount that looks marginal on day one can be very profitable if it acquires a high-CLV traveller.

Changes: whether the marginal first-purchase segments (Spain, Italy, France) are actually worth acquiring, and how much I'll pay to do it.
📊

Contribution margin per booking

The promo cost is clear, the profit it eats is not. Margin differs by market and by hotel versus experiences mix.

Changes: the real breakeven discount depth, and whether a "negative" verdict is mildly or badly negative.
🎯

Sample size & confidence

Without n per cell I can't tell signal from noise, and the France and Italy flags make this urgent.

Changes: whether I roll a finding out, retest it, or ignore it. It decides which rows are safe to act on at all.
📡

Traffic source

A redemption from a coupon or loyalty site is low-incrementality by construction. Source explains a lot of the tenure pattern.

Changes: targeting. I may exclude deal-seeker channels entirely rather than discount the same people repeatedly.
📱

Device & app usage

App and logged-in users skew toward loyal repeat behaviour. Device often proxies for how incremental a booking really is.

Changes: creative and eligibility. I might suppress the offer for high-frequency app users who convert without it.
🔍

Holdout integrity

The France and Italy targeting issue is the biggest unknown. I need the methodology, exposure and any leakage before trusting those rows.

Changes: whether France and Italy get an experiment slot this quarter, or a measurement fix first.
1 Part One · The three experiments I'd run

Only three slots. I'd spend them on learning that pays.

Each one is chosen to answer a question we can act on, and each protects the base while it learns. Together they serve Finance's goal of spending less without losing bookings, and they set up the AI system in Part Two with clean, causal data.

Experiment 01 · Highest saving

Stop paying loyal customers

UK & Germany · Repeat buyers
Offer

Holdout test: 15% versus 0% versus a non-cash perk (free cancellation or loyalty points), matched by cell.

Why this one

Repeat is the clearest value-destroyer (28% and 31% incrementality). It is the biggest, fastest saving available and directly answers Finance.

Expected learning

How much repeat revenue is truly incremental, and whether a perk holds loyalty for less than a discount.

Business risk Some repeat bookings may be incremental. The holdout caps exposure, and I'd watch retention, not just the next booking.
Experiment 02 · Test the hypothesis

Fixed value vs percentage

Germany · First purchase
Offer

A/B a €15 fixed voucher against the 15% discount. On a €102 basket the two cost almost the same, so it is a clean framing test.

Why this one

Qualitative research says Germans prefer fixed value. I'd test it on the proven positive segment, not assume it, and not on repeat buyers.

Expected learning

Whether framing lifts incrementality at equal cost. If it does, it reshapes every DACH offer for free.

Business risk The effect may be small. Cost is matched, so the downside is a quarter's slot, not budget. It also de-risks the Part Three failure.
Experiment 03 · Efficient frontier

How shallow can we go?

Spain · First purchase
Offer

Test discount depth: 10% versus 15%, plus a "€10 off €75+" threshold variant.

Why this one

Spain is marginal (48%) but not flagged, on the lowest basket (€75). It's the cleanest place to find the depth that still acquires.

Expected learning

The discount elasticity for acquisition, which answers Finance directly: can we hold incremental bookings for less?

Business risk Under-discounting could slow acquisition. I'd measure downstream CLV, not just first-booking volume, before scaling.
✋

What I deliberately would not test yet: France and Italy.

Their incrementality is flagged for targeting issues, so any experiment built on that data risks learning the wrong thing. Spending a scarce slot there would be a mistake. I'd fix the holdout first, then earn a slot next quarter. Choosing not to act on broken data is itself a decision.

2 Part Two · The AI operating system

From an idea to an approved launch, without the meeting marathon.

Today a launch waits on manual pulls, hand-drafted specs, sequential reviews and several rounds of meetings. The 60% comes from collapsing that into one assisted pipeline with async approval gates. It's built on CRISP-DM, the framework I used to ship a multi-agent system at Indeed Flex, so business and data understanding come before modelling, which is exactly what most failed enterprise AI skips. 2 Part Two · Two engineering slots

With capacity for only two projects, I'd build one for quality and one for adoption: the spec generator that makes every experiment better, and the dashboard that makes progress visible. Cleverness doesn't decide ROI, adoption and buy-in do. The tempting one is deliberately last, because it is exactly what failed in Part Three.

1

AI experiment specification generator

The biggest bite out of the 60%. It turns a hypothesis plus history into a structured, powered, reviewable spec, replacing the slowest manual step. Build now

2

2 Part Two · Knowledge management

Every experiment should make the next one smarter.

The point of the loop is that learning compounds instead of leaving with whoever ran the test. Capture it in a structured registry, retrieve it before designing anything, and keep a human as the gatekeeper of what counts as knowledge.

📝

Capture

Hypothesis, design, audience, offer, sample size, result (incrementality, CVR, margin), confidence, the decision taken, and caveats like the France targeting issue.

📁

Store

A structured experiment registry for the facts, plus a vector index for the narrative, on one taxonomy of market, segment and offer type.

🔎

Retrieve

Before a new design, AI surfaces similar past experiments and their confidence, so we build on evidence and stop repeating tests.

👤

Validate

A human signs off each learning with a confidence tag and an owner. Contradictions get flagged, and low-confidence entries can't masquerade as fact.

Evidence grade
A · clean holdout B · flawed test C · observed D · hunch

⚡ The one rule that would have caught the German failure: a grade-D hunch can never overrule a grade-A holdout. Evidence quality decides, not recency or seniority.

How I'd keep them honest
Ground them in data, not clichés

Every trait traces to a booking or holdout number, not a national stereotype. If it can't be sourced, it doesn't go in the persona.

Score predictions against reality

Log what the persona predicted, compare it to the live result, and recalibrate. A persona that keeps agreeing with us is a red flag, not a comfort.

Never let it confirm a prior

The persona proposes hypotheses to test. It does not get to bless a decision we'd already made. The holdout is still the judge.

When I'd ignore the persona
When real data contradicts it

A live experiment always beats a simulated one. If the holdout disagrees, the persona is wrong, full stop.

On thin or novel ground

Flagged markets (France, Italy) and untested offer types sit outside what the persona actually knows. Low confidence, low weight.

On high-stakes, one-way doors

For anything expensive or hard to reverse, a persona is a prompt to test, never a substitute for evidence.

2 Part Two · Measuring the system at six months

Did it get faster, better, and more trusted?

Speed on its own is a trap: it is easy to launch worse experiments faster. So I'd pair every efficiency metric with a quality one, and watch adoption and trust as the leading indicators of whether the system actually took hold.

Faster · the headline ask

Speed & adoption

  • Median time from idea to launch, against the 60% target
  • Analyst hours per experiment
  • Share of experiments run through the system
  • Share of specs that are AI-drafted
Better · the quality guardrail

Quality & accuracy

  • Share of experiments that reach significance
  • Share that produce a clear, reusable learning
  • Spec acceptance rate (how much humans edit)
  • QA catch rate, and recommendation hit rate
Worth it · the business case

Impact & trust

  • Net incremental revenue per pound of promo spend
  • Promo spend reduced while bookings hold (Finance's goal)
  • Learnings reused in new specs, and fewer duplicate tests
  • Stakeholder trust score from marketing, PM and Finance

The metric I'd protect hardest is the pairing itself. If time-to-launch drops while the significance rate also drops, the system is manufacturing speed by lowering the bar, and I'd rather catch that early than celebrate a vanity number.

3 Part Three · AI debugging

The engine swapped 15% for a £10 offer in Germany. It flopped. Why?

Before I blame the AI, one thing jumps out. The offer was shown in pounds for a market that pays in euros, and a flat €10 is actually smaller than the €15.30 a 15% discount gave on a €102 basket. So the offer was probably just too small. That's my first suspect. Here are six possible causes, and how I'd check each one.

1

The new offer was simply worth less

A €10 offer (or a literal £10) gives the customer less than the €15.30 that 15% off gave on a €102 basket. People saw a weaker deal, so fewer of them booked. Nothing clever, just a smaller discount.

Investigate

Compare what each offer was actually worth in euros, check which currency the offer was set in, and work out the real discount size in each market.

2

It went to the wrong audience

The "fixed value works" signal came from first-time buyers. If the tool applied it to repeat buyers (only 28% incremental), no offer can save an audience that was already losing money. Right idea, wrong people.

Investigate

Split the live result by audience and confirm who actually received the offer, versus the group the recommendation was based on.

3

It turned one insight into a blanket rule

"German users prefer fixed value" is a single piece of qualitative research. It could be old, based on a few people, or only true for some. The tool treated a hint as a hard fact and rolled it out to everyone.

Investigate

Find the original study's sample size, date and audience, and check whether the preference still holds for the people we applied it to.

4

It backed what looked good, not what drove sales

This is the classic attribution trap. If the tool ranked offers by redemptions or conversion rate instead of the true incremental lift from a holdout, it picked the offer that was simply present when people booked, not the one that actually caused the booking.

Investigate

Check what the tool was told to optimise for, then rerun the pick using incremental lift only and see if the €10 offer still wins.

5

"Worse than expected" against what?

The benchmark itself may be wrong. Germany's neighbours had tracking problems, and seasonality or a competitor's promo could explain the drop just as easily as the offer could.

Investigate

Confirm there was a proper holdout, check the dates for seasonality and competitor activity, and rule out broken tracking or attribution.

6

It guessed, and sounded certain

Fixed-value offers are rarely tested, so the tool had very little data to go on. It made a shaky guess but presented it as a confident recommendation.

Investigate

Check how much fixed-value offer data the tool had to learn from, and whether it produced a confidence score that everyone then ignored.

💡

This is why Part Two is built in that order.

The recommendation engine is powerful, but it fails quietly. A QA check would have caught the currency and discount-size error, and a knowledge base built on incremental lift would have stopped it chasing the wrong metric. Guardrails before autonomy.

4 Part Four · Stakeholder management

Finance wants proof. The Lead wants the waste gone. Both are right.

The disagreement is real and reasonable on both sides. My job is not to pick a winner in the room, it is to turn the argument into a test that protects the base while it settles the question.

Finance

"We've been running this promotion for months and it's delivering bookings. I'm uncomfortable reducing discounts before we have stronger evidence."

Reasonable: they own the risk of a revenue miss.
Incentives Lead · privately

"We're wasting promotional budget on customers who would have booked anyway."

Also reasonable: the incrementality data backs them.
How I'd handle the room 5 moves, not a speech

I wouldn't try to win the argument in the room. Both sides are right, so I'd turn the disagreement into a controlled test and let the data settle it.

  1. 1
    Lead with the data, not a side

    One flat rule is running across two different situations. First-purchase discounts in the UK and Germany are genuinely incremental (63% to 71%); repeat-customer discounts are not (28% to 31%). That reframes it from cut versus keep to "prove which pounds are working."

  2. 2
    Be transparent before the test

    I'd say plainly that the read is directional: France and Italy had holdout targeting issues, and we don't yet have sample sizes or margins. Then agree the success metric and guardrails with both stakeholders up front, so no one relitigates the result later.

  3. 3
    Use sampling to reduce the risk

    A clean holdout on repeat customers in one or two markets, where the discount looks least incremental. The promotion keeps running everywhere else, so Finance's bookings and the base stay protected while we learn. Exposure is capped by design.

  4. 4
    Be transparent after the test

    Same scorecard to both stakeholders next month, one number instead of three. If repeat buyers convert without the discount, we redirect that budget to first-purchase acquisition, where the incremental growth actually is. If they don't, we've lost nothing and learned something real.

  5. 5
    Change nothing we can't measure

    Protect the base, test the assumption, and let a controlled experiment settle the disagreement with evidence rather than opinion. That is how I reduce risk while we keep learning.
